## Key Takeaways

- Medicare is a federal health insurance program primarily for individuals aged 65 and older, but also for some younger people with disabilities or certain conditions.
- There are four parts to Medicare: Part A, Part B, Part C (Medicare Advantage), and Part D (prescription drug coverage).
- Understanding enrollment periods and eligibility requirements is key to avoiding late penalties and ensuring continuous coverage.
- Various changes and updates are expected in 2026, making it essential to stay informed about the latest Medicare developments.

## Understanding Medicare: The Basics

Medicare is a cornerstone of health coverage for millions of Americans. Here's a breakdown of its components:

### Medicare Part A: Hospital Insurance

Medicare Part A covers inpatient hospital stays, skilled nursing facility care, hospice, and some home health care. Most people don't pay a premium for Part A if they have paid Medicare taxes for at least 10 years.

#### Coverage Details

- **Inpatient Care**: Includes semi-private rooms, meals, general nursing, and other hospital services and supplies.
- **Skilled Nursing Facility**: Limited coverage for skilled nursing and rehabilitation services after a hospital stay.
- **Hospice Care**: For terminally ill patients, offering pain relief and support services.
- **Home Health Services**: Limited to medically necessary part-time or intermittent skilled nursing care, physical therapy, and more.

### Medicare Part B: Medical Insurance

Part B covers outpatient care, preventive services, doctor visits, and some home health services. Unlike Part A, Part B requires a monthly premium.

#### Coverage Details

- **Doctor Visits and Outpatient Care**: Includes visits to doctors, outpatient hospital services, and some home health care.
- **Preventive Services**: Flu shots, screenings, and annual wellness visits.
- **Durable Medical Equipment**: Wheelchairs, walkers, and other necessary equipment.

### Medicare Part C: Medicare Advantage

Medicare Advantage (Part C) is an alternative to Original Medicare provided by private insurance companies. These plans often include additional benefits like vision, dental, and prescription drug coverage.

#### Advantages and Considerations

- **Comprehensive Coverage**: Often includes additional services not covered by Original Medicare.
- **Network Restrictions**: May require using network doctors and hospitals.
- **Cost Management**: Plans have an annual out-of-pocket limit, which can offer financial protection.

### Medicare Part D: Prescription Drug Coverage

Part D provides coverage for prescription medications. It's essential to consider a Part D plan if you need regular medications.

#### Details on Enrollment

- **Formulary**: Each plan has a list of covered drugs, known as a formulary.
- **Cost Considerations**: Includes premiums, deductibles, and co-pays.
- **Coverage Gap ("Donut Hole")**: Temporary limit on what the drug plan will cover for drugs, though coverage has improved in recent years.

## Enrollment Periods and Eligibility

Understanding when and how to enroll in Medicare is critical to avoid penalties and coverage gaps.

### Initial Enrollment Period (IEP)

Begins three months before you turn 65, includes the month you turn 65, and ends three months after. Enrolling during this period ensures your coverage starts as soon as you are eligible.

### General Enrollment Period

From January 1 to March 31 each year, for those who missed IEP. Coverage starts on July 1, but late penalties may apply.

### Special Enrollment Period

Available for those who delayed Part B enrollment due to having employer coverage. This period lasts for eight months after employment ends.

### Annual Election Period

From October 15 to December 7, allowing you to make changes to your Medicare Advantage and Part D plans.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between Medicare and Medicaid?

Medicare is a federal program providing healthcare coverage primarily for those over 65 or with specific disabilities, regardless of income. Medicaid is a state and federal program offering health coverage for low-income individuals and families.

### Can I have both Medicare and employer health insurance?

Yes, many people delay enrolling in Part B if they have employer coverage, using Medicare as secondary insurance. It's essential to understand how your current insurance interacts with Medicare to avoid gaps in coverage.

### What happens if I miss my Initial Enrollment Period?

If you miss your IEP, you can enroll during the General Enrollment Period but may face late penalties. It's crucial to enroll on time to avoid additional costs and ensure continuous coverage.
