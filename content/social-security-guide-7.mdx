## Understanding Social Security Basics

### What is Social Security?

Social Security is a government program established in 1935 that provides financial assistance to retirees, disabled individuals, and survivors of deceased workers. It is funded through payroll taxes under the Federal Insurance Contributions Act (FICA).

### How Are Benefits Calculated?

Benefits are calculated based on your highest 35 years of earnings. The Social Security Administration (SSA) uses a formula that considers your Average Indexed Monthly Earnings (AIME) to determine your Primary Insurance Amount (PIA). This amount is what you would receive at your Full Retirement Age (FRA).

### Full Retirement Age (FRA)

Your FRA is the age at which you can receive full retirement benefits. For those born between 1943 and 1954, the FRA is 66, gradually increasing to 67 for those born in 1960 or later.

## Anticipated Changes in 2026

### Cost-of-Living Adjustments (COLA)

Cost-of-Living Adjustments are annual changes made to Social Security benefits to account for inflation. In 2026, these adjustments may see more significant shifts due to economic conditions, impacting the purchasing power of your benefits.

### Retirement Age Adjustments

As life expectancy increases, there is ongoing debate about raising the FRA. By 2026, changes to the FRA could be implemented, potentially affecting when you can start receiving full benefits.

### Earnings Limitations

For those who choose to work while receiving Social Security benefits before reaching FRA, there are earnings limits. Exceeding these limits can result in reduced benefits. In 2026, these limitations might be adjusted, reflecting changes in the economy and workforce dynamics.

## Maximizing Your Social Security Benefits

### Delaying Benefits

One strategy to increase your Social Security benefits is to delay claiming them until age 70. For each year you delay past your FRA, your benefits increase by approximately 8%. This can significantly boost your income during retirement.

### Coordinating Spousal Benefits

Married couples can optimize their benefits by coordinating their claiming strategies. Understanding how spousal benefits work can help maximize the total household income from Social Security.

### Understanding Tax Implications

Social Security benefits may be subject to federal income taxes depending on your overall income. Planning your withdrawals from other retirement accounts can help manage your tax liabilities effectively.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How Can I Apply for Social Security Benefits?

You can apply for Social Security benefits online, by phone, or at your local Social Security office. It's recommended to apply three months before you want your benefits to start.

### What Happens if I Work While Receiving Social Security Benefits?

If you work while receiving benefits before reaching your FRA, your benefits may be temporarily reduced if your earnings exceed certain limits. Once you reach FRA, your benefits will no longer be reduced regardless of your earnings.

### Can I Receive Social Security Benefits if I'm Divorced?

If you were married for at least ten years, you might be eligible to receive benefits based on your ex-spouse's record, provided you are unmarried and meet other criteria. This can be beneficial if your ex-spouse's earnings were higher than yours.
