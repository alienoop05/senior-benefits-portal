## Understanding Social Security

Social Security is a federal program that provides financial assistance to retirees, disabled individuals, and families of retired, disabled, or deceased workers. Funded by payroll taxes, it acts as a safety net, ensuring that workers and their families have a source of income in retirement.

### How Benefits Are Calculated

The amount you receive from Social Security depends on several factors, including your earnings history and the age at which you begin receiving benefits. The Social Security Administration (SSA) calculates your benefits based on your highest 35 years of earnings, adjusted for inflation. It's crucial to ensure that your earnings record is accurate, as errors can lead to reduced benefits.

### Full Retirement Age

For individuals born in 1960 or later, the full retirement age (FRA) is 67. However, you can start receiving benefits as early as age 62, albeit at a reduced rate. Delaying benefits until age 70 can increase your monthly payments significantly. Understanding your FRA is crucial in strategizing when to start receiving benefits to maximize your income during retirement.

## Changes to Social Security in 2026

As with any major program, Social Security undergoes periodic changes to adapt to economic and demographic shifts. Here's what you need to know about changes coming in 2026:

### Increase in Full Retirement Age

The gradual increase in the full retirement age is a significant change affecting future retirees. While the FRA for those born in 1960 and later is 67, any further changes will require close monitoring, as they may impact when you choose to retire and claim benefits.

### Adjustments to Cost-of-Living Allowances (COLA)

Social Security benefits are adjusted annually for inflation through Cost-of-Living Allowances. In 2026, expect potential recalibrations in how COLA is calculated, possibly affecting the purchasing power of your benefits.

### Changes to Taxation of Benefits

Currently, Social Security benefits can be taxable based on your combined income. Any legislative changes in 2026 could alter the tax landscape, so it's prudent to stay informed and consult with a tax professional to understand how these changes may impact you.

## Strategies to Maximize Your Social Security Benefits

Maximizing your Social Security benefits requires careful planning and consideration of several factors. Here are some strategies to consider:

### Delaying Benefits

One of the most effective ways to increase your Social Security benefits is to delay receiving them until age 70. For every year you delay past your full retirement age, your benefits increase by approximately 8%. This strategy can significantly boost your retirement income, especially if you have a longer life expectancy.

### Understanding Spousal Benefits

Married couples have unique opportunities to maximize their benefits through spousal benefits. A spouse is entitled to receive up to 50% of the other spouse's full retirement benefit. Coordinating when each spouse claims benefits can optimize the total amount received over time.

### Considering Taxes

Social Security benefits can be subject to federal income tax, depending on your total income. It's essential to understand the tax implications and explore strategies to minimize taxes on your benefits, such as managing withdrawals from other retirement accounts strategically.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the earliest age I can start receiving Social Security benefits?

You can start receiving Social Security benefits as early as age 62. However, doing so will result in reduced monthly payments compared to waiting until your full retirement age.

### How are Social Security benefits taxed?

Social Security benefits may be taxable if your combined income exceeds certain thresholds. Up to 85% of your benefits can be subject to federal income tax. It's important to consult with a tax professional to understand how taxes may affect your benefits.

### Can I work while receiving Social Security benefits?

Yes, you can work while receiving Social Security benefits. However, if you are below your full retirement age and earn more than a certain amount, your benefits may be temporarily reduced. Once you reach full retirement age, there is no penalty for working and receiving benefits simultaneously.
