## What Are Social Security Benefits and Who Is Eligible?

Social Security benefits provide financial assistance to retirees, disabled individuals, and families of deceased workers. To qualify, you need to have earned enough "credits" through your work history, typically requiring about ten years of work. The benefits are calculated based on your 35 highest-earning years. If you haven't worked that long, zeros are averaged in, which can lower your benefit amount.

### Types of Social Security Benefits

1. **Retirement Benefits**: Available as early as age 62, though claiming early can reduce your monthly benefits. Full retirement age (FRA) varies depending on your birth year, gradually increasing to 67 for those born in 1960 or later.
   
2. **Disability Benefits**: For those unable to work due to a severe medical condition expected to last at least a year or result in death. Eligibility requires meeting a strict definition of disability and having earned sufficient work credits.
   
3. **Survivor Benefits**: Paid to family members of deceased workers, including widows, widowers, and dependent children. Eligibility depends on the deceased's work history and the survivor's relationship to the deceased.

## Strategies to Maximize Your Social Security Benefits

Maximizing Social Security benefits can significantly impact your retirement income. Here are some strategies to consider:

1. **Delay Retirement**: Waiting until age 70 to claim benefits can increase your monthly payout due to delayed retirement credits, which add approximately 8% per year after reaching FRA.
   
2. **Spousal Benefits**: If you're married, you might be eligible for spousal benefits, which can equal up to 50% of your spouse's FRA benefit. This is particularly beneficial if your work history is shorter or your lifetime earnings are lower.
   
3. **Consider Working Longer**: By working longer, you can replace lower-earning years with higher-earning ones, increasing your benefit. Additionally, continuing to earn can boost your Social Security credits.
   
4. **Coordinate With Your Spouse**: If both you and your spouse qualify for benefits, coordinating when each of you claims can maximize household income.

## Anticipated Changes to Social Security in 2026

As Social Security faces financial challenges, adjustments are likely to ensure the program's sustainability. While details are speculative, potential changes include:

- **Increased Payroll Taxes**: To bolster the Social Security trust fund, there might be an increase in payroll taxes or the earnings cap for taxes.
- **Benefit Formula Adjustments**: Modifications to the benefit formula could impact the monthly payments, particularly for higher earners.
- **Eligibility Age Changes**: There's a possibility of raising the early or full retirement age, impacting when individuals can start claiming benefits.

## Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Avoiding common Social Security mistakes can help ensure you receive the benefits you're entitled to:

- **Claiming Too Early**: While tempting, claiming benefits early can permanently reduce your monthly income.
- **Not Considering Spousal Benefits**: Many overlook the potential advantages of spousal benefits, which can enhance retirement income.
- **Ignoring Tax Implications**: Depending on your total income, up to 85% of your Social Security benefits may be taxable. Plan accordingly to avoid surprises.
- **Failing to Review Your Earnings Record**: Regularly checking your Social Security statement for accuracy ensures you're not missing out on benefits due to unreported earnings.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How can I find out my expected Social Security benefits?

You can access your Social Security Statement online at the Social Security Administration's website. It provides a detailed record of your earnings and an estimate of your future benefits.

### What happens if I continue to work while claiming Social Security?

If you're below full retirement age and earn more than the annual limit, your benefits may be temporarily reduced. However, after reaching full retirement age, there are no penalties for working.

### Can I collect Social Security benefits from my ex-spouse?

Yes, if you were married for at least ten years, are currently unmarried, and your ex-spouse is eligible for benefits, you can claim based on their earnings record without affecting their benefits.

### How are Social Security benefits taxed?

Social Security benefits may be taxed based on your combined income. If your income exceeds certain thresholds, up to 85% of your benefits could be taxable.

### What should I do if there's an error in my Social Security record?

Contact the Social Security Administration immediately to correct any discrepancies in your earnings record. Accurate records are crucial for calculating your benefits correctly.
